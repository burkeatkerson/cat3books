faqItems:
  - question: "What is the IICRC?"
    answer: "The Institute of Inspection, Cleaning and Restoration Certification (IICRC) is the internationally recognized certification and standard-setting body for the cleaning, inspection, and restoration industries. IICRC develops technical standards (S500 for water damage, S520 for mold, S700 for fire and smoke, and others) and administers professional certifications (WRT, ASD, AMRT, FSRT, CCT, OCT, CDS, RRT, and others). Insurance carriers, TPA programs, and property owners use IICRC certification as a baseline competency indicator for restoration contractors. IICRC-certified firms must maintain insurance, have certified technicians on staff, and comply with IICRC's code of ethics. Source: iicrc.org"
  - question: "What is WRT certification in restoration?"
    answer: "Water Damage Restoration Technician (WRT) is the foundational IICRC certification for restoration technicians performing water damage response, mitigation, and structural drying. WRT covers: water damage science, the drying process, category and class of water, safety requirements, documentation, and basic psychrometrics. WRT is considered the entry-level restoration certification — most TPA programs and carrier preferred vendor agreements require all technicians to be WRT-certified. Prerequisites: none (entry-level). Exam cost: $200–$400. Annual maintenance: CEUs required for renewal. Source: iicrc.org"
  - question: "What is ASD certification in restoration?"
    answer: "Applied Structural Drying (ASD) is an IICRC certification for restoration professionals who have mastered advanced structural drying science — including psychrometrics, advanced drying systems, equipment selection and placement, and drying monitoring. ASD builds on WRT fundamentals with deeper technical content on the science of moisture movement, material science, and drying system design. ASD is typically required for senior technicians, lead technicians, and project managers. Prerequisite: WRT certification. Exam cost: $400–$700. Source: iicrc.org"
  - question: "What is AMRT certification and when is it required?"
    answer: "Applied Microbial Remediation Technician (AMRT) is the IICRC certification for professionals performing mold and mold-related microbial remediation. AMRT covers the IICRC S520 standard — mold identification, health effects, containment protocols, remediation methods, and post-remediation verification. AMRT is required by many carriers, property managers, and TPA programs as a condition of performing mold remediation on insurance claims. Prerequisites: WRT or RRT. Exam cost: $400–$600. Source: iicrc.org"
  - question: "What is the IICRC S500 standard?"
    answer: "IICRC S500 Standard for Professional Water Damage Restoration is the industry technical standard governing all aspects of water damage mitigation: water damage categories (clean, gray, black water), classes of water damage (1–4, by evaporation load), drying science, documentation requirements (drying logs, moisture maps, psychrometric readings), and post-drying verification. IICRC S500 is the primary basis for what constitutes 'professional' water damage restoration — its documentation requirements define what carriers expect to see in claim files. Compliance with S500 is foundational to billing defensibility."
  - question: "What is the difference between IICRC and RIA certifications?"
    answer: "IICRC (Institute of Inspection, Cleaning and Restoration Certification) focuses on technician-level certifications and technical standards for specific restoration disciplines. IICRC certifications are held by individual technicians and indicate competency in specific technical skills (water damage, mold, fire, etc.). RIA (Restoration Industry Association) is a trade association that offers professional designations for restoration industry professionals at a business and management level: CR (Certified Restorer), WLS (Water Loss Specialist), FLS (Fire Loss Specialist), and others. RIA designations recognize broader professional achievement, including business knowledge, ethics, and industry leadership — complementing (not replacing) IICRC technical certifications."
  - question: "What is EPA RRP certification and why does it matter for restoration?"
    answer: "EPA RRP (Renovation, Repair, and Painting) certification is required by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency for contractors performing renovation, repair, or painting activities that disturb lead-based paint in residential properties built before 1978 — when there is a child under 6 or a pregnant woman present, or in child-occupied facilities. In restoration, EPA RRP applies when remediation work in older homes disturbs lead paint during demo. RRP requires: firm certification, certified renovator on-site, lead-safe work practices, documentation of disturbance area and disposal, and post-work cleaning verification. Fines for non-compliance can be significant."
  - question: "What OSHA requirements apply to restoration contractors?"
    answer: "OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration) requirements applicable to restoration contractors include: Hazard Communication (HazCom / GHS) for chemical safety, Respiratory Protection (29 CFR 1910.134) for mold and fire work, Personal Protective Equipment (29 CFR 1910.132) for hazardous conditions, Bloodborne Pathogen standards for Category 3 water (sewage) losses, confined space entry standards for sewage or utility work, and general industry fall protection requirements. Restoration involves hazardous conditions (wet environments, structural instability, mold, smoke contamination, sewage) that create OSHA obligations. OSHA inspections often follow significant injury events; maintaining a safety program reduces both injury risk and regulatory exposure."
  - question: "What is NADCA certification and when is it relevant to restoration?"
    answer: "NADCA (National Air Duct Cleaners Association) is the trade association and certification body for HVAC system inspection and cleaning. The NADCA ACR (Assessment, Cleaning, and Restoration) standard governs the proper cleaning of HVAC systems affected by construction debris, mold, or smoke contamination. After fire or smoke events, HVAC cleaning to NADCA ACR standards is often required by carriers as part of the claim. NADCA's VSMR (Ventilation System Mold Remediator) certification is specifically relevant to restoration companies cleaning HVAC systems affected by mold during water damage events."

<DefinedTerm term="ABRA" fullName="American Bio Recovery Association" summary="The trade association for biohazard, crime scene, and trauma remediation contractors — setting standards for Category 3 water and trauma cleanup." seeAlso="IICRC, NORMI">
The **American Bio Recovery Association (ABRA)** is the trade association and standards body for the biohazard remediation, crime scene cleanup, and trauma recovery industry. ABRA publishes standards for Category 3 water cleanup (sewage, contaminated water), biohazard remediation, and body recovery cleanup. ABRA membership and certification (Certified Bio Recovery Technician, CBRT) is relevant to restoration companies handling Category 3 water losses, hoarding cleanup, or meth lab remediation. Source: [americanbiorecovery.org](https://americanbiorecovery.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="AIHA" fullName="American Industrial Hygiene Association" summary="The professional organization for industrial hygienists — relevant to restoration through air quality testing standards and post-remediation verification protocols." seeAlso="IICRC, NORMI">
The **American Industrial Hygiene Association (AIHA)** is the professional organization for industrial hygienists — scientists and professionals who anticipate, recognize, evaluate, and control environmental health hazards in the workplace. In restoration, AIHA standards are relevant to: post-mold remediation clearance testing (AIHA-accredited labs analyze air samples), indoor air quality assessments after fire and smoke events, and occupational exposure evaluation for restoration workers. Industrial hygienists often serve as third-party clearance inspectors on mold remediation projects. Source: [aiha.org](https://www.aiha.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="AMRT" fullName="Applied Microbial Remediation Technician" summary="IICRC certification for professionals performing mold and microbial remediation — covers IICRC S520 standards, containment protocols, and post-remediation verification." seeAlso="WRT, IICRC, IICRC S520, NORMI">
**Applied Microbial Remediation Technician (AMRT)** is an IICRC certification for restoration professionals specializing in mold and microbial remediation. AMRT covers: microbiology basics, health effects of mold exposure, recognition and identification of mold damage, IICRC S520 remediation protocols, containment setup and management, remediation methods, post-remediation verification procedures, and documentation requirements. AMRT is required by many TPA programs and carriers as a condition of performing mold remediation on insurance claims. Prerequisite: WRT or RRT certification. Cost: $400–$600 for exam and certification. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="ASD" fullName="Applied Structural Drying" summary="Advanced IICRC certification covering the science of moisture movement, psychrometrics, advanced drying systems, and monitoring — required for senior restoration technicians." seeAlso="WRT, IICRC, IICRC S500">
**Applied Structural Drying (ASD)** is an IICRC certification that builds on WRT fundamentals to cover advanced structural drying science: detailed psychrometrics, moisture movement through building materials, advanced drying equipment selection and placement, large-loss drying systems, monitoring protocols, and drying documentation. ASD technicians can design and optimize drying systems for complex losses — large water intrusions, multiple floors, wet concrete, building envelope moisture, and challenging psychrometric conditions. ASD is typically required for lead technicians, crew supervisors, and project managers in restoration. Prerequisite: WRT certification. Cost: $400–$700. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<h2 id="letter-c">C</h2>

<DefinedTerm term="CCT" fullName="Contents Cleaning Technician" summary="IICRC certification for professionals specializing in cleaning, restoring, and managing personal property damaged by water, fire, or mold." seeAlso="IICRC, OCT, IICRC S500">
**Contents Cleaning Technician (CCT)** is an IICRC certification for restoration professionals specializing in the cleaning, restoration, and management of personal property (contents) affected by water damage, fire, smoke, mold, or other covered perils. CCT covers: contents assessment, cleaning methods by material type, drying and deodorizing techniques, pack-out procedures, documentation requirements, and insurance billing for contents work. CCT provides the professional standard that justifies per-item cleaning charges in insurance claims — carriers expect CCT-documented procedures in contents claim files. Cost: $300–$500. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="CDS" fullName="Commercial Drying Specialist" summary="An advanced IICRC certification for restoration professionals managing large-scale commercial drying projects — covering complex building systems and large-loss protocols." seeAlso="ASD, WRT, IICRC">
**Commercial Drying Specialist (CDS)** is an advanced IICRC certification designed for restoration professionals specializing in large-scale commercial drying projects — including high-rise buildings, complex HVAC systems, concrete structures, and large-loss events. CDS covers: commercial building systems, structural materials science, advanced psychrometrics for large spaces, desiccant and mechanical refrigeration equipment, large-loss project management, and coordination with building engineers and facility managers. CDS certification is relevant to restoration companies targeting commercial and large-loss work. Prerequisite: ASD certification. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="CR" fullName="Certified Restorer" summary="The RIA's flagship professional designation — the highest-level professional credential in the restoration industry, recognizing comprehensive knowledge of restoration science, business, and ethics." seeAlso="RIA, WLS, FLS, CLS">
**Certified Restorer (CR)** is the Restoration Industry Association's flagship professional designation — the highest-level individual credential in the restoration industry. CR recognizes comprehensive knowledge across restoration science, business management, ethics, and professional standards. Eligibility requires: significant years of restoration industry experience, passing a comprehensive examination, completing required CEU hours, and agreeing to the RIA's code of ethics. CR designation signals to carriers, TPA programs, and property owners that the restorer has achieved a comprehensive, validated level of professional competency. Source: [restorationindustry.org](https://www.restorationindustry.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="CLS" fullName="Contents Loss Specialist" summary="RIA designation recognizing expertise in contents restoration — pack-out, cleaning, valuation, storage, and settlement of personal property claims." seeAlso="RIA, CR, CCT">
**Contents Loss Specialist (CLS)** is an RIA professional designation for restoration professionals with demonstrated expertise in contents restoration — the assessment, cleaning, valuation, storage, and settlement of personal property damaged in insured losses. CLS requires knowledge of: contents assessment methodology, IICRC CCT cleaning standards, item-by-item valuation approaches, pack-out and storage management, insurance settlement procedures, and total loss documentation. CLS complements (but is not the same as) IICRC's CCT certification — CLS is an RIA business-level designation while CCT is an IICRC technical certification. Source: [restorationindustry.org](https://www.restorationindustry.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<h2 id="letter-e">E</h2>

<DefinedTerm term="EPA RRP" fullName="EPA Renovation, Repair, and Painting Rule" summary="Federal regulation requiring certified firms and renovators for work disturbing lead-based paint in pre-1978 residential and child-occupied facilities." seeAlso="OSHA">
The **EPA Renovation, Repair, and Painting (RRP) Rule** (40 CFR Part 745) requires that contractors performing renovation, repair, or painting in pre-1978 residential dwellings and child-occupied facilities (where a child under 6 is present, or in schools and daycare) must be: (1) certified renovator firms (firm certification, renewed every 5 years), (2) staffed with at least one certified renovator on each job site, and (3) compliant with lead-safe work practices (containment, cleaning, and post-work verification). In restoration, RRP applies to demolition and remediation in pre-1978 homes where lead paint is disturbed. Penalties for non-compliance: up to $37,500 per violation per day (civil) with potential criminal penalties. Source: [epa.gov/lead](https://www.epa.gov/lead)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="ERS" fullName="Environmental Remediation Specialist" summary="RIA designation for restoration professionals with expertise in environmental remediation — mold, hazardous materials, indoor air quality, and regulatory compliance." seeAlso="RIA, AMRT, CR">
**Environmental Remediation Specialist (ERS)** is an RIA professional designation recognizing expertise in environmental remediation within the restoration context — including mold remediation, hazardous material handling, indoor air quality assessment, and regulatory compliance. ERS designation is relevant to restoration companies with significant mold, Category 3 water (sewage), or specialty remediation practices. Source: [restorationindustry.org](https://www.restorationindustry.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<h2 id="letter-f">F</h2>

<DefinedTerm term="FLS" fullName="Fire Loss Specialist" summary="RIA designation recognizing expertise in fire and smoke restoration — structural cleaning, contents restoration, deodorization, and insurance claims management for fire losses." seeAlso="RIA, CR, FSRT">
**Fire Loss Specialist (FLS)** is an RIA professional designation for restoration professionals with expertise in fire and smoke damage restoration — including structural cleaning, deodorization, HVAC cleaning, contents assessment, smoke damage evaluation, insurance claims management for fire losses, and coordination with public adjusters on complex fire claims. FLS designation complements IICRC's FSRT technical certification. Source: [restorationindustry.org](https://www.restorationindustry.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="FSRT" fullName="Fire and Smoke Restoration Technician" summary="IICRC certification covering fire damage assessment, structural cleaning, smoke and soot remediation, deodorization, and documentation." seeAlso="IICRC, OCT, IICRC S700">
**Fire and Smoke Restoration Technician (FSRT)** is an IICRC certification for restoration professionals specializing in fire and smoke damage restoration. FSRT covers: fire damage assessment, combustion chemistry and smoke behavior, structural cleaning methods for different smoke and soot types, content cleaning and evaluation, deodorization techniques (thermal fogging, ozone, hydroxyl), HVAC system cleaning, documentation and photography standards, and safety requirements for fire-contaminated environments. FSRT certification is typically required for technicians performing fire restoration work on insurance claims. Cost: $300–$500. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="HST" fullName="Health and Safety Technician" summary="IICRC certification covering health and safety requirements for restoration technicians — chemical safety, PPE, emergency procedures, and regulatory compliance." seeAlso="IICRC, OSHA">

<h2 id="letter-i">I</h2>

<DefinedTerm term="IICRC" fullName="Institute of Inspection, Cleaning and Restoration Certification" summary="The internationally recognized certification and standard-setting body for the inspection, cleaning, and restoration industries — publisher of S500, S520, S700, and other restoration standards." seeAlso="RIA, WRT, ASD, AMRT, FSRT, CCT, OCT">
The **Institute of Inspection, Cleaning and Restoration Certification (IICRC)** is the internationally recognized standard-setting and certification body for the cleaning, inspection, and restoration industries. IICRC develops technical standards (S500, S520, S540, S700) that define professional practice and develops and administers professional certifications (WRT, ASD, AMRT, FSRT, CCT, OCT, CDS, RRT, JRT, and others). IICRC certification is used by insurance carriers and TPA programs as a baseline competency indicator. IICRC-certified firms must maintain qualifying insurance, employ certified technicians in relevant specialties, and follow the IICRC code of ethics. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="IICRC S500" fullName="IICRC Standard for Professional Water Damage Restoration" summary="The technical standard governing all aspects of professional water damage mitigation — water categories, classes, documentation, drying science, and verification." seeAlso="IICRC, WRT, ASD, Drying Log">
**IICRC S500 Standard for Professional Water Damage Restoration** is the primary technical standard governing water damage mitigation practice. S500 defines: water damage categories (Category 1 — clean water, Category 2 — gray water, Category 3 — black water/sewage), water damage classes (Class 1–4, based on evaporation load and extent of absorption), drying science and protocols, required documentation (drying logs, moisture maps, psychrometric readings), post-drying verification standards, and health and safety considerations. S500 compliance is the basis for claiming that restoration work was performed professionally — carriers expect S500-compliant documentation in water damage claim files. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="IICRC S520" fullName="IICRC Standard for Professional Mold Remediation" summary="The technical standard governing professional mold remediation — assessment, containment, removal, cleaning, and post-remediation verification." seeAlso="IICRC, AMRT, NORMI">
**IICRC S520 Standard for Professional Mold Remediation** governs all aspects of professional mold remediation practice: mold assessment and sampling, worker health and safety, containment design and management, remediation methods by material type (porous, semi-porous, non-porous), post-remediation verification (PRV) protocols, and documentation requirements. S520 compliance provides the scientific and procedural basis for mold remediation billing — carriers expect S520-documented procedures and post-remediation clearance verification in mold claim files. AMRT certification is aligned with S520 standards. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="IICRC S540" fullName="IICRC Standard for Trauma and Crime Scene Remediation" summary="The technical standard governing professional cleanup of traumatic events, crime scenes, and human biological contamination." seeAlso="IICRC, ABRA">
**IICRC S540 Standard for Trauma and Crime Scene Remediation** governs professional practice for the remediation of biological contamination associated with traumatic events, crime scenes, suicides, unattended deaths, and infectious disease decontamination. S540 covers: bloodborne pathogen standards, PPE requirements, containment and transport of biological waste, state and federal regulatory compliance, documentation requirements, and clearance testing. S540 is relevant to restoration companies accepting Category 3 or trauma/biohazard work. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="IICRC S700" fullName="IICRC Standard for Professional Fire and Smoke Damage Restoration" summary="The technical standard governing professional fire and smoke restoration — damage assessment, structural cleaning, deodorization, and documentation." seeAlso="IICRC, FSRT, OCT">
**IICRC S700 Standard for Professional Fire and Smoke Damage Restoration** governs the professional practice of fire and smoke damage restoration: fire damage assessment methodology, types of smoke and soot residues and their behavior, structural cleaning methods by substrate type, HVAC system cleaning standards, deodorization methods, contents assessment and restoration, post-restoration verification, and documentation requirements. S700 provides the professional and technical basis for fire restoration billing — carriers expect S700-compliant documentation in fire claim files. FSRT certification aligns with S700 standards. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<h2 id="letter-j">J</h2>

<DefinedTerm term="JRT" fullName="Journeyman Restorer Technician" summary="An entry-level IICRC certification pathway for restoration technicians completing structured apprenticeship programs." seeAlso="IICRC, WRT">
**Journeyman Restorer Technician (JRT)** is an IICRC certification pathway designed for restoration technicians who are participating in structured apprenticeship or on-the-job training programs. JRT provides a formal credential for technicians who are gaining restoration skills through workplace training rather than formal classroom coursework. The JRT pathway allows employers to track technician development toward full IICRC certifications. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)

<h2 id="letter-n">N</h2>

<DefinedTerm term="NADCA" fullName="National Air Duct Cleaners Association" summary="The trade association and standard-setting body for HVAC inspection and cleaning — relevant to restoration for post-fire, post-mold, and post-construction HVAC cleaning." seeAlso="IICRC, FSRT">
The **National Air Duct Cleaners Association (NADCA)** is the trade association and standard-setting body for the HVAC inspection, cleaning, and restoration industry. NADCA publishes the ACR (Assessment, Cleaning, and Restoration) standard for HVAC system cleaning — the primary professional standard used by carriers and property managers when requiring HVAC cleaning after fire, mold, or construction contamination events. NADCA's ASCS (Air Systems Cleaning Specialist) and VSMR (Ventilation System Mold Remediator) certifications are relevant to restoration companies offering HVAC cleaning as a service. Source: [nadca.com](https://www.nadca.com)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="NORMI" fullName="National Organization of Remediators and Mold Inspectors" summary="An alternative certification and training body for mold assessment and remediation professionals." seeAlso="IICRC, AMRT, AIHA">
**NORMI (National Organization of Remediators and Mold Inspectors)** is an alternative certification and training organization for mold inspection and remediation professionals. NORMI certifications include: CMR (Certified Mold Remediator) and CMA (Certified Mold Assessor). NORMI is distinct from and independent of IICRC — its certifications are recognized by some states and carriers, but IICRC AMRT is more widely recognized in the insurance restoration context. Restoration companies should confirm which certifications their specific carrier and TPA relationships recognize before pursuing NORMI credentials as primary qualifications.
</DefinedTerm>

<h2 id="letter-o">O</h2>

<DefinedTerm term="OCT" fullName="Odor Control Technician" summary="IICRC certification for professionals specializing in odor identification and remediation — smoke, mold, Category 3 water, and other malodor sources." seeAlso="IICRC, FSRT, S700">
**Odor Control Technician (OCT)** is an IICRC certification covering professional odor identification and remediation. OCT content includes: odor science (sources, types, behavior), deodorization equipment (thermal foggers, hydroxyl generators, ozone generators, activated carbon filtration), application techniques for different odor types (smoke, mold, Category 3 water, pet, decomposition), material-specific treatment approaches, safety considerations for deodorizing chemicals and equipment, and documentation requirements. OCT certification supports billing for deodorization services in fire, mold, and Category 3 water claims. Cost: $250–$400. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="OSHA" fullName="Occupational Safety and Health Administration" summary="The federal regulatory agency that sets and enforces workplace health and safety standards — multiple OSHA standards apply directly to restoration work." seeAlso="EPA RRP">
**OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration)** is the federal agency responsible for workplace health and safety regulation under the Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970. Key OSHA standards applicable to restoration work include: Hazard Communication (HazCom/GHS, 29 CFR 1910.1200), Respiratory Protection (29 CFR 1910.134), Personal Protective Equipment (29 CFR 1910.132), Bloodborne Pathogens (29 CFR 1910.1030) for Category 3 water and biohazard work, and General Industry Walking-Working Surfaces. OSHA inspections can be triggered by worker injury reports or regulatory complaints; maintaining documented safety programs, equipment inspection records, and training records is the primary defense against OSHA citations. Source: [osha.gov](https://www.osha.gov)
</DefinedTerm>

RIA provides: education (conferences, webinars, technical training), industry advocacy with carriers and legislators, the Cost of Doing Business Report (the primary financial benchmark publication for restoration companies), professional designations (CR, WLS, FLS, CLS, ERS), and networking through local chapters and national events. RIA membership signals professional commitment to the industry and provides access to resources, contacts, and carrier relationship development. <DefinedTerm term="RRT" fullName="Registered Restorer Technician" summary="An intermediate IICRC credential recognizing restoration technicians who have completed multiple certifications but have not yet achieved full Certified Restorer (CR) designation." seeAlso="IICRC, CR, WRT, ASD">
**Registered Restorer Technician (RRT)** is an intermediate IICRC credential for restoration professionals who have completed multiple certifications but have not yet met all requirements for the Certified Restorer (CR) designation. RRT can serve as a prerequisite for certain advanced IICRC certifications (including AMRT). RRT recognition by specific carriers and TPA programs varies — some recognize it as a qualification level, others do not specifically reference it. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<h2 id="letter-w">W</h2>

<DefinedTerm term="WLS" fullName="Water Loss Specialist" summary="RIA designation recognizing expertise in water damage restoration — technical, operational, and insurance claims management." seeAlso="RIA, CR, WRT, ASD, FLS">
**Water Loss Specialist (WLS)** is an RIA professional designation for restoration professionals with specialized expertise in water damage loss management — including technical drying science, IICRC S500 standards implementation, insurance claims management for water losses, supplement strategy, documentation best practices, and carrier communication. WLS complements IICRC technical certifications (WRT, ASD) with the business and insurance claims competencies that RIA designations emphasize. Source: [restorationindustry.org](https://www.restorationindustry.org)
</DefinedTerm>

<DefinedTerm term="WRT" fullName="Water Damage Restoration Technician" summary="The foundational IICRC certification for water damage restoration — covering water categories and classes, basic drying science, psychrometrics, and documentation requirements." seeAlso="IICRC, ASD, AMRT, IICRC S500">
**Water Damage Restoration Technician (WRT)** is the foundational IICRC certification for restoration technicians performing water damage response and mitigation. WRT covers: the science of water damage, water damage categories (1–3) and classes (1–4), the drying process and psychrometrics basics, safety requirements for water damage environments, documentation requirements (moisture readings, drying logs), equipment selection and deployment, and customer communication. WRT is considered the baseline certification for any restoration technician — most TPA programs and carrier preferred vendor agreements require all technicians to hold WRT. No prerequisites. Cost: $200–$400. Source: [iicrc.org](https://iicrc.org)
</DefinedTerm>

## Certification Pathway: Common Progression for Restoration Technicians

| Experience Level | Recommended Certifications |
|---|---|
| Entry-level (0–1 year) | WRT (required baseline) |
| Experienced technician (1–3 years) | WRT + ASD + FSRT (if doing fire work) |
| Lead technician / crew supervisor | WRT + ASD + AMRT (if mold work) + OCT |
| Project manager | Above + CCT (if contents) + CDS (if commercial large-loss) |
| Business owner / senior professional | RIA WLS, FLS, or CLS + RIA CR (highest designation) |
