## The Argument From Cost

The most common objection to specialized restoration bookkeeping is price: generic bookkeeping costs less, and if the books are "close enough," the savings justify the difference.

This argument only holds if you count only the fee and ignore what the gaps cost.

Generic bookkeeping for a restoration company creates five categories of measurable value erosion. Each has a specific dollar range. Each is preventable. And each compounds over time — meaning the cost of choosing generic bookkeeping increases the longer the arrangement continues.

This post quantifies each category, builds a composite "true cost" model, and shows what the comparison looks like when you count both sides of the ledger.

---

## The True Cost Model: Five Categories

The five cost categories below aren't theoretical projections. They're based on the gap patterns consistently found when transitioning restoration companies from generic to specialized bookkeeping. The dollar ranges are conservative estimates based on industry patterns and are calibrated to a $2M restoration company. They scale up roughly proportionally with revenue.

---

## Cost Category 1: Unbilled and Uncollected Supplements

**What it is:** Supplement revenue that was either never submitted, submitted but not tracked through approval, or approved but never collected.

**Why it happens:** Supplement tracking requires a systematic workflow: log the submission, note the adjuster contact, follow up on approval status, reconcile the payment against the approved scope. Generic bookkeepers don't have this workflow because they're not part of the supplement cycle — they see payments when they arrive, not the upstream process that produces them. Approved supplements that don't arrive as payments look the same as payments that haven't arrived yet. Without active tracking, the uncollected supplement looks like a timing difference — and the money is never chased.

**The mechanics of the loss:** A restoration company generating $2M in annual revenue might submit $300,000–$500,000 in supplements across all jobs in a year. Industry data from RIA member surveys suggests that contractors using systematic supplement tracking collect 85–92% of submitted supplement scope. Contractors without systematic tracking collect 65–75%. The gap — 10–20 percentage points on $300,000–$500,000 in annual supplemental scope — is $30,000–$100,000 in uncollected revenue per year. The conservative estimate for a typical $2M company: **$15,000–$35,000 per year**.

<Claim label="Supplement Claim">
A $2M restoration company submitting supplements on 40–60% of jobs without a systematic supplement tracking workflow loses an estimated $15,000–$35,000 per year in approved-but-uncollected supplemental revenue — not from scope denial, but from tracking failure.
</Claim>

**What the books show:** Revenue line is smooth and doesn't spike with supplement approvals. AR aging has large persistent balances that can't be attributed to specific approval stages. Supplement revenue is indistinguishable from base job revenue — because it's booked the same way when it arrives, with no connection to the submission that preceded it.

**What specialized bookkeeping does:** Maintains a supplement ledger by job: date submitted, adjuster contact, expected amount, approval status, approval date, payment received, variance from approved amount. Every open supplement is a tracked receivable. Monthly close includes a supplement reconciliation — how many submitted, how many approved, how many paid, how many outstanding, how many denied.

## Cost Category 2: TPA Fee Misclassification

**What it is:** Third-Party Administrator fees (Code Blue, Contractor Connection, Alacrity, Sedgwick, Worley, and others) that are either not recorded, recorded incorrectly, or buried in a way that makes program-level profitability invisible.

**Why it happens:** TPA fees reduce the gross revenue a contractor receives. When a $100,000 job is referred through a program with a 15% takedown, the contractor receives $85,000 — not $100,000. If the bookkeeper records $100,000 in revenue and a $15,000 "referral fee" expense, the gross margin on that job looks like it's based on $100,000 in revenue, which is wrong. The contractor's actual revenue on that job is $85,000. The margin percentage is calculated on the wrong base.

Alternatively — and more commonly — the bookkeeper records $85,000 in revenue and ignores the TPA fee entirely, because that's what the contractor actually received. The result: the P&L doesn't show what the job was worth at full rate, and there's no record of what was paid to the TPA program.

Neither treatment produces the number that matters: program-specific net margin, comparable across all TPA programs, to enable the keep-or-drop decision.

**The mechanics of the distortion:** Consider a $2M restoration company generating 40% of revenue through TPA programs, at an average 13% takedown. That's $800,000 in TPA-routed revenue and approximately $104,000 in TPA fees per year. If those fees are misclassified — not broken out by program, not connected to the specific jobs they affected — the owner has no visibility into which programs are costing the most relative to the volume they generate.

The dollar cost of this misclassification isn't a direct revenue loss — it's a decision distortion. Owners who can't see program-specific profitability make program participation decisions based on volume instead of margin. They keep high-volume, low-margin programs and underinvest in direct marketing that would generate the same volume at higher margins. Over time, the margin erosion from this misdirection is **$10,000–$20,000 per year** in foregone margin — conservative relative to the volume involved.

<Claim label="TPA Claim">
A restoration company with 40% of revenue through TPA programs and no program-specific P&L tracking is making program participation decisions without the data those decisions require — with an estimated annual margin impact of $10,000–$20,000 from retained low-margin programs that should have been dropped or renegotiated.
</Claim>

**What the books show:** A single "TPA fees" line in the cost section with one total — no breakdown by program, no connection to job-level revenue. Or no TPA line at all — the fees are netted from deposits.

**What specialized bookkeeping does:** Creates a named account per active TPA program in the chart of accounts. Every TPA fee is coded to its program. Monthly P&L shows cost by program; job-level P&L shows the specific fee for each TPA-routed job. The Code Blue Test — gross margin per Code Blue job vs. gross margin per Contractor Connection job — becomes a reportable number. *See [The Code Blue Test: How to Decide Which TPA Programs to Drop](/field-notes/code-blue-test-tpa-programs).*

## Cost Category 3: Revenue Recognition Errors

**What it is:** Revenue recognized in the wrong period because ACV, RCV, and supplement payments are booked using inconsistent timing rules.

**Why it happens:** Revenue recognition in restoration is genuinely complex. The correct approach depends on whether the company uses cash-basis or accrual-basis accounting, and within accrual, how the insurance billing cycle maps to performance obligations under ASC 606. Most restoration companies use some form of hybrid — formally cash-basis for simplicity, but with accruals applied inconsistently when large items are involved.

A generic bookkeeper defaults to recognition on receipt (cash basis) but may accrue large items at period end without consistent logic. The result: some revenue is recognized when received, some when earned, some when estimated. This creates tax years that don't accurately reflect either cash position or economic reality.

**The mechanics of the exposure:** Consider a restoration company that completes a $300,000 job in November. The carrier pays the ACV of $225,000 in December (current tax year). The supplement for $35,000 is approved in January (following tax year). The RCV holdback of $40,000 is released in February (following tax year).

Under cash basis: $225,000 in Year 1, $75,000 in Year 2. Clear.
Under accrual with consistent recognition: $300,000 in Year 1 when the performance obligation is complete. Clear.
Under inconsistent treatment: depends on when the bookkeeper decides to accrue each item. Common error: accrue the $300,000 in Year 1 (because the job is "done") but also recognize the supplement as additional income in Year 2 when it's received. Result: $300,000 in Year 1 + $35,000 in Year 2 = $335,000 recognized on a $300,000 job. The extra $35,000 is phantom income in Year 2.

**The estimated annual exposure:** For a $2M restoration company with 15–25 jobs per year showing some version of timing misalignment, the revenue recognition exposure is typically **$20,000–$50,000 in a given tax year** — not because that much revenue disappears, but because it lands in the wrong year and creates phantom income or deferred income that generates unexpected tax liability. The direct cash cost: at a 25–30% combined federal and state tax rate on phantom income, **$5,000–$15,000 in incremental annual tax liability** from timing errors alone.

<Claim label="Revenue Recognition Claim">
Revenue recognition inconsistencies — mixing cash-basis and accrual treatment across ACV, supplement, and RCV payments — create an estimated $5,000–$15,000 in annual incremental tax liability for a $2M restoration company, plus balance sheet distortions that make cash position unreadable.
</Claim>

**What the books show:** Revenue recognition notes in the tax workpapers that describe inconsistent treatment year-over-year. A CPA who has to "normalize" revenue each year to get to taxable income. Surprising tax bills in years with large supplement activity.

**What specialized bookkeeping does:** Applies a consistent, explicitly documented revenue recognition policy — either cash-basis with clear rules, or accrual with the ACV/supplement/RCV timeline mapped to when each obligation is earned and collectible. The policy is documented, applied consistently, and coordinated with the CPA so tax years are predictable.

## Cost Category 4: Valuation Discount at Sale

**What it is:** The reduction in sale price that buyers and lenders apply when a company's financial records don't support the quality-of-earnings confidence they need.

**Why it happens:** When a restoration company goes to market — whether for a bank line expansion, private equity acquisition, or strategic sale — the buyer or lender conducts quality-of-earnings analysis. This analysis asks: do the reported earnings reflect real, recurring economic performance? Can the revenue be traced to specific jobs and carriers? Are the cost categories correct? Is the margin trend reliable?

Companies with clean, job-costed, supplement-tracked, TPA-segregated books answer all of these questions with clear data. Companies with generic books cannot — the data doesn't exist in the right structure. The buyer or lender responds with a quality-of-earnings discount, typically expressed as a reduction in the EBITDA multiple applied to the reported earnings.

**The mechanics of the discount:** At $500,000 in EBITDA and a base multiple of 5×, the enterprise value is $2.5M. A 1× quality-of-earnings discount (the low end for companies with significant bookkeeping gaps) reduces the multiple to 4× and the enterprise value to $2.0M — a $500,000 reduction in sale price. A 1.5× discount reduces it to $1.75M — a $750,000 reduction.

Industry data from restoration M&A advisors suggests that current multiples for $3M–$10M EBITDA restoration companies range from 5× to 8×. Quality-of-earnings discounts on companies with poor books range from 0.5× to 2.0× EBITDA. **The expected valuation discount for a company with three or more years of generic bookkeeping and no job-level P&L is $150,000–$500,000** when the transaction eventually occurs.

<Claim label="Valuation Claim">
A $2M restoration company selling at a 5× EBITDA multiple with messy books will receive a quality-of-earnings discount of 0.5–1.5× EBITDA — reducing the transaction price by $125,000–$375,000 compared to a company with the same EBITDA and clean, job-costed financial records.
</Claim>

**What the books show:** Financial statements that require extensive reconstruction in due diligence. Revenue that can't be traced to specific jobs. Margin data that doesn't separate by service line or TPA program. An AR balance that can't be explained by a clear job-level breakdown. A CPA who has been doing reconstructive normalization for years to produce usable tax returns.

**What specialized bookkeeping does:** Produces financial records that survive buyer due diligence without significant reconstruction. Job-level P&L, supplement tracking, TPA program profitability, AR by payment stage — all of these are the specific outputs that quality-of-earnings analysts look for. Clean books command clean multiples. *See [Should You Accept That PE Acquisition Offer?](/field-notes/should-you-accept-pe-acquisition-offer)*

## Cost Category 5: Working Capital Tied Up in Untracked AR

**What it is:** Cash that should be in your account — from collected receivables — that isn't, because your AR management doesn't distinguish between receivables ready to collect and receivables that need approval, documentation, or carrier action.

**Why it happens:** Restoration AR has four distinct stages: ACV pending (sent to carrier, awaiting payment), supplement pending approval (submitted, in adjuster review), RCV holdback pending release (work complete, documentation submitted, awaiting holdback release), and genuinely overdue (payment should have arrived, needs follow-up). Generic bookkeeping AR aging treats all four as undifferentiated receivables over 30/60/90/120 days.

When your AR aging shows $150,000 over 90 days, you don't know: how much is ACV from recent large jobs that just hasn't arrived yet (normal), how much is a supplement submitted six months ago that needs follow-up (recoverable), how much is an RCV holdback from a job you completed and documented (check in), and how much is a carrier that's simply slow-paying (chase it). Without that breakdown, the follow-up strategy can't be targeted, and the cash that could be collected continues to sit.

**The working capital impact:** For a $2M restoration company with $200,000 in average AR — not unusual for a business with 60–90 day payment cycles — **$30,000–$75,000 is typically recoverable faster** than the current collection timeline, if the AR is properly staged and pursued. The difference in working capital availability represents the opportunity cost of poor AR tracking: either the business draws on its line of credit unnecessarily, or growth opportunities are passed because the cash doesn't appear to be available.

<Claim label="AR Claim">
A $2M restoration company with an undifferentiated AR aging report — no breakdown by ACV stage, supplement approval stage, or RCV holdback stage — has an estimated $30,000–$75,000 in working capital trapped in receivables that could be collected faster with proper staging and follow-up.
</Claim>

**What the books show:** Large AR aging balances with no explanation of why they're aging. A credit line that's perpetually drawn despite consistent revenue. An inability to predict which months will be good cash months vs. tight cash months — because the AR collection timeline is opaque.

**What specialized bookkeeping does:** Stages AR by payment type and tracks each receivable against its expected collection timeline. ACV receivables get flagged if they age past 45 days. Supplements are tracked from submission date. RCV holdbacks are monitored against job completion documentation. The monthly AR report tells the owner what to collect now, what to follow up on, and what to wait for — and why. *See [Building a 13-Week Cash Forecast for Restoration](/field-notes/13-week-cash-forecast-restoration).*

## But Won't a Good Generic Bookkeeper Close These Gaps Over Time?

The counterargument: with enough experience in your company, a generic bookkeeper will learn the restoration-specific mechanics and close the gaps.

This is possible but systematically unlikely, for three reasons.

**First, they don't know which gaps to close.** The gaps listed above are invisible from the bookkeeper's perspective — they're applying correct general-business accounting. They won't know to separate ACV from RCV because they don't know the distinction matters. They won't know to track supplements by submission status because the supplements they see are payments, not submissions. The knowledge gaps create the blind spots; the blind spots prevent self-correction.

**Second, the learning curve is long and the error rate during it is high.** A generic bookkeeper learning restoration mechanics on the job will get some things right and some things wrong. The errors during the learning period compound — three months of improperly recognized revenue recognition, six months of untracked supplements. By the time the bookkeeper has learned enough to do it correctly, the cleanup cost equals or exceeds the savings from their lower fee.

**Third, experience in your company is specific, not transferable.** If the generic bookkeeper leaves, you start over. Specialized bookkeepers bring industry knowledge that isn't dependent on company-specific training — they know the mechanics before they start.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does generic bookkeeping actually cost a restoration company?

For a typical $2M company: $75,000–$175,000 in annual value erosion across the five categories. The service fee is $9,600–$18,000/year. Total annual cost: $84,600–$193,000. Specialized bookkeeping costs $18,000–$36,000/year in fees with gaps eliminated. The total cost comparison almost always favors specialization.

### What's the biggest single hidden cost?

For companies planning to sell: the valuation discount, which arrives as a lump sum at the transaction — $150,000–$500,000 or more. For companies not planning to sell: the combination of unbilled supplements and TPA margin distortion, typically $25,000–$55,000 per year for a $2M company.

### Can I recover these costs retroactively?

Partially. Supplements from the last 12 months may still be recoverable. Unclaimed RCV holdbacks can be audited and claimed if the work was documented. Prior-period tax errors may require amended returns. Valuation discounts can be partially reduced by 12–24 months of clean books before a sale process. The faster you address the gaps, the more recovery is available.

### How do I quantify the cost of my current generic bookkeeping?

Four audits: (1) Compare supplement submissions to supplement payments for the last 12 months — the gap is your unbilled supplement estimate. (2) Calculate TPA revenue and fees paid by program — if these aren't in your books, run the math manually. (3) Ask your CPA whether revenue recognition is consistent year-over-year. (4) If you've had conversations with buyers or lenders, ask whether they flagged book quality. Any concerns indicate where the gaps are largest.

### Is this problem specific to large restoration companies?

No. The same gaps appear at $500K as at $5M — the dollar impact scales with revenue. A $500K company losing 1.5% of revenue to uncollected supplements loses $7,500/year. A $5M company loses $75,000/year. The relative impact on owner economics is often larger at smaller companies.

### What if I'm planning to sell in the next three to five years?

Start now. Quality-of-earnings analysis at sale looks back 3–5 years. Buyers discount years they can't rely on. Three years of clean, job-costed, supplement-tracked books produced before the sale process begins is the difference between commanding a strong multiple and accepting a QoE discount. The longer the clean record, the less the discount.

### How do I know if my AR tracking is causing working capital problems?

Diagnostic: run your AR aging and try to explain each balance over 60 days by carrier, job, and payment stage. If you can't — if the aging report just shows totals — your AR tracking doesn't have enough resolution to support active management. A properly structured AR report for restoration can tell you, for every balance: what it is, what stage it's at, and what action would resolve it.

### What does "unbilled supplement" actually mean?

A supplement is unbilled when it was approved by the adjuster but the payment wasn't pursued — either because the carrier never sent it and nobody followed up, or because the approval was captured in the estimate system but not in the billing workflow. Approved supplements that aren't collected are permanently lost after the carrier closes the file — typically 18–24 months after the job close date.

### Does this cost model apply to mitigation companies specifically?

The same five cost categories apply, with different proportions. Mitigation companies typically have higher equipment revenue risk (equipment days frequently go unbilled) and lower supplement complexity than full-service restorers, but the TPA fee misclassification and working capital impacts are similar. See [Why Mitigation Companies Have Unique Accounting Needs](/field-notes/mitigation-companies-unique-accounting-needs) for the mitigation-specific breakdown.

### How does a specialized bookkeeper prevent these costs?

Systematic workflows for each gap category: supplement ledger tracking, TPA cost accounts by program, consistent revenue recognition policy, AR staging by payment type, and equipment-day reconciliation. These aren't sophisticated — they're specific. They require knowing what workflows to build, which requires restoration industry knowledge that generic bookkeepers don't have on day one.

### If I switch to a specialized bookkeeper now, how long until I see the financial impact?

First 30–60 days: chart of accounts and workflow setup. Days 60–90: first full close under the new system, with supplement ledger and TPA coding in place. Month 3–6: first supplement recovery cycle visible in the books. Month 6–12: first year-over-year margin comparison by job type and TPA program. Year 2+: the full financial picture of what was being missed, compared to what's now being captured.
