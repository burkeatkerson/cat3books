## The Question We Get on Every Albi Onboarding

"I just signed up for Albi. Does this replace QuickBooks?"

The short answer is no. The longer answer requires understanding what Albi actually is, what it does in your accounting workflow, and what it doesn't do — because the gap between what owners expect from the integration and what it actually provides is exactly where the books break down.

---

## What Albi Actually Is

Albi was built specifically for the restoration industry — not adapted from a generic field service platform. It shows. The platform understands that a water job has drying cycles, equipment days, supplement milestones, and ACV/RCV stages in a way that generic job management platforms don't.

**What Albi does:**
- Claim intake and dispatch management
- Field documentation (photos, notes, drying logs)
- Equipment tracking and equipment-day logging
- Cost tracking by job (materials, equipment, subcontractors)
- Supplement milestone workflow
- TPA program dispatch and communication
- Job profitability reporting (operational, not accounting)
- QBO integration (data transfer to your accounting system)

**What Albi doesn't do:**
- Revenue recognition
- AR management (aging, staging, collections tracking)
- Payroll processing or labor allocation
- Month-end financial close
- Tax reporting
- The financial reports your bank or a buyer would require

### The Integration Is a Starting Point, Not a Finish Line

The Albi → QBO sync is the cleanest out-of-the-box integration we've worked with among restoration job management platforms. But "out-of-the-box" doesn't mean "no setup required."

Before the sync produces useful accounting data, someone needs to:
1. Map Albi's cost categories to the correct QBO accounts
2. Set up QBO income accounts by service line so Albi revenue posts to the right place
3. Configure TPA program cost accounts
4. Decide whether to use QBO Projects or the sub-customer hierarchy for job tracking

This is a one-time configuration project — typically 4–8 hours of setup work by a bookkeeper who knows both systems. Once it's done, the sync runs automatically. If it's skipped, the sync runs but posts transactions to wrong accounts.

## What Doesn't Move: The Three Gaps

### Gap 1: Payroll and Labor Allocation

Albi tracks time entries for field employees, but it doesn't process payroll. Your payroll platform (Gusto, ADP, QBO Payroll) processes and pays employees. The gap: field labor hours coded to specific jobs in Albi need to be reconciled against actual payroll amounts and allocated to QBO job costs.

This reconciliation is a monthly step that takes 30–45 minutes for most operations. It requires someone who understands loaded labor rates — because the correct job-cost figure is not the employee's gross wages but their loaded rate (wages × burden factor of 1.30–1.55). See [The Complete Guide to Job Costing for Restoration](/field-notes/complete-guide-job-costing-restoration-mitigation) for loaded rate calculation.

### Gap 2: Supplement Lifecycle Tracking

Albi has a supplement tracking workflow — you can log supplement submissions and milestone updates. What doesn't automatically flow to QBO: the staged AR treatment of supplements. A properly tracked supplement has:
- A QBO invoice created when the supplement is carrier-approved (not submitted)
- That invoice aged separately from ACV receivables
- Payment applied when the check arrives

Albi can feed this workflow with the data it has. But the QBO work — creating the invoice at the right stage, aging it separately, applying the payment correctly — is bookkeeping work that happens in QBO, not in Albi.

### Gap 3: AR Staging by Payment Type

Restoration AR isn't one number — it's three: ACV pending, RCV holdback pending, and supplements pending. A properly structured QBO setup has AR aging that separates these. Albi doesn't produce this view. It's a QBO function, and it requires a bookkeeper who knows to build it.

## The Three-Layer Model

The best-performing $1M–$5M restoration financial setups we work with use a three-layer model:

**Layer 1: Albi** — job management, dispatch, documentation, field workflow, cost tracking. Where the operational data lives.

**Layer 2: QuickBooks Online** — financial records, AR, AP, payroll, month-end close, financial reporting. Where the accounting happens.

**Layer 3: Specialized restoration bookkeeper** — configures and maintains the Albi → QBO integration, performs month-end close, reconciles AR by payment stage, manages supplement tracking, and produces the owner's monthly financial package.

The bookkeeper is the layer that makes the other two produce useful financial data. Without it, you have operational data in Albi and general-business data in QBO — neither of which tells you which jobs are profitable, which TPA programs are worth keeping, or whether your AR is being collected on time.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does Albi replace QuickBooks?

No — they're complementary. Albi handles job operations; QBO handles accounting. The integration automates the data bridge between them.

### What's the most important thing to configure in the Albi → QBO integration?

The cost account mapping — making sure Albi's cost categories post to the correct QBO accounts. Without this, job costs land in the wrong places and your job P&L is meaningless.

### How does Albi handle supplements?

Albi tracks supplement milestones operationally. The accounting treatment — creating AR, staging it separately, applying payment — is QBO work. Albi feeds the data; QBO holds the accounting.

### Is Albi good for reconstruction companies?

Yes, though reconstruction introduces job complexity (longer duration, more subcontractors, draw billing) that requires the WIP accounting layer in QBO that reconstruction introduces. Albi handles the job management side; the WIP accounting happens in QBO.
